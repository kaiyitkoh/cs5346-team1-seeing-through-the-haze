Seeing through the Haze

Every year, a thick haze settles over Singapore. It travels thousands of kilometers from fires burning in Indonesia's soggy, carbon-rich soil (called peatlands) and forests.

This is the story of where the haze comes from and why some years are much worse than the others.

The Pattern

Between 2014 and 2024, Singapore usually had about 7 "unhealthy" days (or Haze Days) a year, where the air quality, measured by the Pollutant Standards Index (PSI), went above 100.

Most years pass with little incident, but when conditions align, the numbers will shoot up.

2015 was the worst year in a decade.

Singapore suffered through 46 haze days, with the peak 24-hour PSI reading hitting a dangerous 322, well into the "hazardous" range. Schools were shut, outdoor work was stopped, and hospitals were filled with people struggling to breathe.

Interestingly, the haze follows a seasonal pattern.

From June to September, during the monsoon season, the Southwest Monsoon winds act like a giant fan, blowing haze from Sumatra straight toward Singapore. In the chart showing the daily PSI readings, nearly every major spike falls within the monsoon season.

These hazy days always seem to happen during the monsoon season.
But where do the fires come from?
The Source

The answer is right across the sea in neighbouring Indonesia.

Indonesia is the main source. We ranked provinces by how much they impact us (Impact Score) by dividing the fire detection count by the distance to Singapore.

It revealed that a nearby province with fewer fires can impact Singapore's air more than a faraway one with thousands of fires.

Riau province alone accounts for nearly half of all impact on Singapore's air quality.

Even though South Sumatra has far more fires, Riau is only 279km away. Being so close means its smoke hits us much harder.

Distance is the secret factor.

Johor (Malaysia) is only 101 km away, so even a few fires (272) there matter. Central Kalimantan has over 18,000 fire detections, but it is so far away (1,115km) that the smoke rarely reaches us.

Fire detection counts are based on the Visible Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite (VIIRS), a NASA satellite sensor that detects fire hotspots at 375m resolution. Data from the Suomi NPP and NOAA-20 satellites underpins the fire counts in this analysis.

The Rain Myth

You might think rain washes the haze away, but the data shows this is not true.

Our analysis shows that local rain has almost no power to clear the air. It only explains 3% of why the haze leaves after a spike in PSI. If the fires are still burning and the wind is still blowing, rain won't save us.

The fires are in Indonesia.
But what makes some years so much worse than others?
The Climate Driver

The number of fires in Indonesia changes drastically every year.

In quiet years, satellites see fewer than 500 fires. In the worst years, they see more than 23,000. Rainfall alone does not explain these massive jumps.

The key is a weather cycle in the Pacific Ocean.

We track a pattern called El Niño, which occurs when the Oceanic Niño Index (ONI) rises to +0.5 or above. When it happens, Southeast Asia becomes much hotter and drier, making it very easy for fires to spread.

The pattern is striking.

In El Niño years, highlighted in red, fire counts spike dramatically. The massive El Niño in 2015 coincided with Singapore's worst haze crisis.

This weather cycle in the Pacific Ocean is called the El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO). It is the #1 reason why some years have terrible haze.

The 2015 crisis was staggering.

Indonesia recorded over 23,000 fires, double any other year. The extreme drought turned the ground into a "tinderbox" that caught fire easily, with fires peaking in October with nearly 10,000 detections.

El Niño makes things worse.
But something shifted after 2020.
What Changed

Before 2020, Singapore's annual peak PSI was a roller coaster, with the worst readings recorded during El Niño years.

Since 2020, the worst haze days have become much milder, dropping by 66 points on average.

This was not a one-time dip. The milder pattern remained consistent, with only a minor uptick during an El Niño year in 2023.

So, was it new laws, the COVID pandemic, or just better weather? The answer is likely "all of the above".

Indonesia tightened peatland regulations significantly after the 2015 crisis. President Jokowi established the Peatland Restoration Agency (BRG) in 2016, tasked with restoring 2.49 million hectares of degraded peatland. A permanent moratorium on new peatland permits followed in 2019.

COVID reduced economic activity, and pandemic-era restrictions in Indonesia likely curtailed land-clearing operations as well.

A wet weather pattern called "La Niña" (marked by the ONI index being below -0.5), kept the region damp and harder to burn.

To test whether the improvement was real, we built a predictive model trained on fire activity, wind, and the El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) weather cycle.

It classifies each day into risk tiers and suggests that the post-2020 improvement is structural, not just a run of good weather. You can explore day-by-day predictions in the Explorer tool.

What the data ruled out

Did the haze drop only because of COVID lockdowns? We checked the data, and the answer is no.

The air is cleaner because there are fewer fires being lit, not just because people stayed at home (mobility data has zero predictive power after controlling for fire counts).

The haze is not random.

It is a predictable cycle driven by fires in nearby places like Johor and Riau, carried by monsoon winds, and made worse by dry El Niño years. Rainfall does not help as much as people think. When the wind blows haze from burning peatlands towards Singapore, even wet weather cannot wash the air clean.

Because we understand these factors, we can now predict "High Risk" days before the haze even reaches Singapore.

  • Distance and Wind: How close a fire is what matters most. Because Johor and Riau are nearby, the Southwest Monsoon winds may blow its smoke directly to us, hitting Singapore's air quality the hardest.
  • The El Niño Factor: During dry El Niño years, the number of fires dramatically increases, creating the perfect conditions for a major haze crisis.
  • The Post-2020 Drop: The cleaner air since 2020 is real. It is likely a mix of better laws, wetter weather, and changes caused by the pandemic.
